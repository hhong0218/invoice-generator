Web Developer Invoice Template

Web development billing spans fixed-bid projects, hourly sprints, recurring hosting, and maintenance retainers — often all for the same client. This guide shows developers how to structure each on an invoice clients understand and approve, and you can generate one free in the browser.

Billing models for developers

How you invoice depends on how you scoped the work, and most developers use more than one model:

  • Fixed-bid projects. A single agreed price for a defined build. Enter it as one line, or break it into milestones (design sign-off, backend, launch) so payment is tied to progress.
  • Time and materials. Hourly or daily billing for ongoing or open-scope work. Put your rate in the unit price and hours in the quantity column, ideally with one line per feature or ticket so the client can see where time went.
  • Retainers. A recurring monthly fee for a block of support or development hours. A single recurring line you reuse each cycle keeps it simple.
  • Pass-through costs. Hosting, domains, third-party APIs, or premium plugins you purchase on the client’s behalf — list these separately so they’re not mistaken for your labour.

Milestone invoicing for projects

For anything beyond a small build, milestone billing protects both sides. Common splits are a deposit to start (often a third), a payment at a key checkpoint such as staging sign-off, and the balance at launch. Issue an invoice per milestone, reference the same project name and number scheme on each, and note what the payment unlocks. This keeps cash flowing and gives the client a clear sense of progress.

Recurring revenue: hosting and maintenance

Maintenance retainers and managed hosting are the most valuable lines a developer can build, because they recur. Make the scope explicit — uptime monitoring, security updates, a set number of support hours, backups — so the client understands the ongoing value rather than seeing a mystery monthly charge. Set the invoice to issue on the same day each month and keep the line item description consistent for easy reconciliation.

Handling expenses and third-party costs

When you front costs like a year of hosting, a paid API tier, or a stock asset, list each as its own line at cost (or with your agreed markup) and label it clearly. Mixing these into your hourly total makes invoices look inflated and complicates the client’s bookkeeping. Some developers prefer the client pay vendors directly; if you front the cost, the invoice is the clean record of reimbursement.

Payment terms that suit development work

Net–15 or net–30 are typical, with deposits up front for project work. State accepted payment methods — bank transfer, card, or a payment link — and any late-payment policy in the notes. Make sure every invoice has a unique number, issue and due dates, your and the client’s details, itemized lines, subtotal, tax if applicable, and the total due. Frequently asked questions

Should I bill a deposit before starting a build?

Yes — for fixed-bid projects an upfront deposit (commonly a third) is standard. It covers your initial work and signals the client is committed. Invoice it before you begin and show it as a credit on the final milestone invoice.

How do I invoice hosting and maintenance?

Use a recurring monthly line with a clear scope — updates, monitoring, backups, support hours. Issue it on the same date each month and keep the description consistent so the client can reconcile it easily.

How should I list third-party costs?

List each pass-through cost (hosting, APIs, plugins) on its own line at cost or with your agreed markup, separate from your labour. This keeps the invoice transparent and simplifies the client’s bookkeeping.

What payment terms work for development?

Net–15 or net–30 are common, with deposits for project work and milestone payments for larger builds. Always state a real due date and accepted payment methods on the invoice.
