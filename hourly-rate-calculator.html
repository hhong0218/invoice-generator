What is a freelance hourly rate calculator?

Most freelancers set their rate by guessing, copying a number from a forum, or matching what a former employer paid per hour — none of which account for the real costs of running an independent business: gaps between projects, unpaid admin time, vacation, and expenses you no longer have covered by an employer. This calculator uses your actual income goal and time constraints to work out the rate that gets you there.

How it's calculated

The formula follows the standard approach used by freelance platforms and rate-setting guides:

  • Billable weeks per year = 52 − (vacation days + holidays + sick days) ÷ 5 (time off converted from days to weeks, assuming a 5-day work week)
  • Billable hours per week = Total working hours per week − non-billable hours per week
  • Hourly rate = (Desired annual income + Annual business expenses) ÷ (Billable hours per week × Billable weeks per year)

In other words: figure out the total amount of money you need the business to bring in over the year (your take-home pay plus what it costs to run the business), then divide that by the number of hours you'll actually be able to bill across the year — not the number of hours you're "at work."

Why billable hours are always less than total hours

A common mistake is dividing income by 2,080 hours (40 hours × 52 weeks), which assumes you bill every single working hour of the year with zero vacation, sick time, or admin work. In practice, most freelancers can only bill 60-75% of their working hours once you subtract marketing, invoicing, client communication, learning, and time between contracts — which is exactly why this calculator asks for those separately rather than assuming they don't exist.

What counts as a "business expense" here?

Include anything the business pays for that a salaried employee wouldn't have to think about: software subscriptions, a portion of your health insurance, equipment, a coworking desk or home office costs, accounting or bookkeeping fees, and self-employment tax you set aside. This calculator treats your "desired annual take-home income" as the amount you want left over after those costs — not your total invoiced revenue.

Frequently asked questions

How many hours can I realistically bill per week?

Most freelancers can bill somewhere between 20 and 30 hours out of a 40-hour week once admin, marketing, and client communication are subtracted — this calculator lets you enter your own estimate for non-billable hours so the result matches your actual workflow rather than an unrealistic 100% billable assumption.

Should I include taxes in my expenses?

Yes, if you want your "desired income" number to represent what actually lands in your pocket. Self-employed workers typically need to set aside 25-30% of profit for federal, state, and self-employment taxes (this varies by location and situation), so many freelancers include an estimated tax set-aside in their business expenses to keep the take-home figure accurate.

What if I can't get enough clients to bill this many hours?

This calculator tells you the rate you need at a given number of billable hours — if the market won't support that rate, the usual options are to raise your rate less aggressively and increase billable hours (take on more clients), reduce your target income, or cut business expenses. It's a planning tool, not a guarantee the market will pay any specific number.

Does this account for time between contracts?

Indirectly, yes — the "non-billable hours per week" and "vacation/time off" fields let you build in a buffer for slow periods. If you expect significant downtime between projects, increase your non-billable hours or reduce your assumed billable weeks to reflect that.

Is my data uploaded anywhere?

No. This calculator runs entirely in your browser using plain JavaScript. Nothing you enter is sent to a server or stored.
