Designer Invoice Template

Design work is rarely a single flat fee — there are concepts, revision rounds, source files, and licensing to account for. This guide shows designers exactly how to structure an invoice that gets approved without a back-and-forth, and you can build one free in the browser.

How designers should structure an invoice

The biggest reason design invoices get questioned is vague line items. A client who sees one line that reads “Design work — $3,000” has nothing to map against the project they agreed to. Break the work into the phases you actually quoted: discovery, concepts, revisions, and final delivery. When each stage is its own line, the invoice doubles as a summary of what was delivered.

A clean designer invoice usually includes:

  • Project or campaign name so the client’s accounts team can match it to a brief or purchase order.
  • Line items per deliverable — logo concepts, brand guidelines, a set of social templates, a landing-page mockup — rather than one lump sum.
  • Revision rounds, with included rounds noted and any extra rounds billed separately so scope creep is visible.
  • Source files and handoff as a distinct line if you charge for editable files (AI, PSD, Figma) or a packaged asset library.
  • Usage or licensing terms, especially for logos and illustration, where the fee often depends on how broadly the work will be used.

Hourly, flat fee, or value-based?

Most independent designers bill one of three ways. Hourly works for open-ended retainer or production work — put your rate in the unit price and hours in the quantity column. Flat project fees suit defined deliverables like a logo or a website; enter the agreed amount with a quantity of one and describe the scope in the line. Value-based pricing ties the fee to the impact of the work and is also entered as a flat line. You can mix them on one invoice — a flat brand-identity fee plus a few hourly lines for out-of-scope edits.

Deposits and milestone billing

For projects over a few thousand dollars, bill a deposit before you start — commonly 30–50% — and the balance on delivery. Issue the deposit as its own invoice, then show it as a credit line on the final invoice so the client sees the remaining balance clearly. Milestone billing (a payment at concept approval, another at delivery) keeps cash flowing on longer engagements and protects you if a project stalls.

Protecting your work until you’re paid

Many designers add a short note stating that copyright and usage rights transfer to the client only once the invoice is paid in full. This is standard practice and gives you leverage on late payments without souring the relationship. Put your payment terms (net–14 or net–30 are common for design), accepted methods, and any late fee in the notes section of every invoice.

What to include before you send

Double-check that your invoice has a unique number, the issue and due dates, your business and the client’s details, itemized deliverables, subtotal, tax if you charge it, and the total due. Frequently asked questions

Should I charge extra for source files?

Many designers do. Editable files (Figma, AI, PSD) and packaged asset libraries are deliverables clients can build on, so it’s reasonable to list them as a separate line or bundle them into a higher tier. Decide this up front and state it in your quote so the invoice matches expectations.

How many revision rounds should I include?

Two to three rounds is typical for most design projects. Note the number included in your line item and bill additional rounds at an hourly or per-round rate. Making this visible on the invoice keeps scope creep in check.

When does copyright transfer to the client?

A common arrangement is that usage and copyright transfer only after the invoice is paid in full. State this in your invoice notes and contract. It protects you on late payments while keeping terms clear and professional.

Do I need to charge sales tax on design work?

It depends on your location and whether design services are taxable there. In many U.S. states services are exempt but tangible deliverables may not be; in the EU, VAT usually applies. Check your local rules and add the tax rate on the invoice when required.
