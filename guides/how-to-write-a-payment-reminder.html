How to Write a Payment Reminder Email (with Templates)

An unpaid invoice usually isn't a sign of a bad client — it's a sign your email got buried. A good payment reminder is short, specific, and easy to act on. This guide shows you exactly when to send each reminder, how to shift your tone as an invoice ages, and gives you copy-paste templates for every stage.

When to send each reminder

Timing matters as much as wording. Sending too early feels pushy; waiting too long lets the invoice slide to the bottom of the pile. A reliable cadence looks like this:

  • A few days before the due date — an optional, friendly heads-up.
  • On or 1-3 days after the due date — a gentle "this may have slipped" nudge.
  • 7-14 days late — a firmer, more direct follow-up.
  • 30 days late — a formal final notice that references your terms.

The key is consistency. Decide on a cadence and apply it to every client, so following up is a routine rather than an awkward decision you agonize over each time.

What every reminder should include

No matter the stage, a strong reminder always contains the same core facts so the client can pay without writing back to ask questions:

  • The invoice number and the original due date.
  • The exact amount due.
  • A clear how-to-pay instruction — a payment link or bank details.
  • The invoice PDF reattached, so they never have to hunt for it.

Let your tone follow the calendar

The single most useful idea here is a tone progression. Early reminders assume the best — people are busy and emails get lost. As the invoice ages, your language becomes more direct, but it stays professional throughout. You are never angry; you are simply clearer about consequences.

Stage 1 — Before the due date (optional, friendly)

This pre-emptive note is goodwill, not pressure. It quietly puts the due date back on the client's radar.

Subject: Invoice INV-0042 — due Friday, July 3

Hi Sam,

Just a quick heads-up that invoice INV-0042 for $1,200 is due this Friday, July 3. The invoice is attached again for convenience, and you can pay by bank transfer or the link at the bottom.

Thanks so much — happy to help if you have any questions.

Best,
Alex

Stage 2 — On the due date / 1-3 days late (gentle nudge)

Assume it simply slipped. Keep it warm and make paying effortless.

Subject: Friendly reminder — invoice INV-0042 ($1,200)

Hi Sam,

I hope you're well. This is a friendly reminder that invoice INV-0042 for $1,200, due July 3, is now showing as outstanding. It may simply have slipped through — no problem at all.

I've reattached the invoice here. You can pay by bank transfer or the payment link inside. If it's already on its way, please ignore this note.

Thanks again for your business!

Best,
Alex

Stage 3 — 7-14 days late (firm but polite)

Now you name the overdue status plainly and ask for a specific commitment — a payment date.

Subject: Overdue: invoice INV-0042 — please advise on payment

Hi Sam,

I'm following up on invoice INV-0042 for $1,200, which was due on July 3 and is now 10 days overdue. I haven't received payment or a reply to my earlier note, so I want to make sure it didn't get lost.

Could you let me know when I can expect payment? If there's an issue with the invoice or you need anything from me to process it, just tell me and I'll sort it out today.

The invoice is attached again. Thanks for your prompt attention.

Best,
Alex

Stage 4 — 30 days late (final notice)

This is direct and references your terms and any consequences. It is still professional, but it sets a clear deadline.

Subject: Final notice — invoice INV-0042 now 30 days overdue

Hi Sam,

Despite my previous reminders, invoice INV-0042 for $1,200 remains unpaid and is now 30 days past its July 3 due date. Please treat this as a formal final notice.

I'd like to resolve this without further steps. Kindly arrange full payment by July 31. Per the terms on the invoice, a late fee of 1.5% per month may apply to balances unpaid after this date, and I may need to pause any further work until the account is settled.

If you've already paid, please send the remittance details so I can reconcile it. Otherwise, I'm happy to discuss a short payment plan — just reply and let me know.

Regards,
Alex

When to escalate beyond email

If the final notice deadline passes with no response, you've exhausted the friendly route. At that point, your options include applying the late fee you stated on the invoice, pausing or withholding any deliverables or future work, sending a formal demand letter, or — for larger amounts — using a collections service or a small-claims process. Escalation is far easier when your original invoice clearly stated the due date and late-payment terms, which is exactly why those details belong on every invoice from the start.

A few habits that prevent reminders entirely

The best reminder is the one you never have to send. Invoice the moment work is delivered, state a specific calendar due date instead of just "Net 30," include payment instructions directly on the invoice, and confirm you're sending to the right billing contact. These small habits resolve most slow payments before a single follow-up is needed.
