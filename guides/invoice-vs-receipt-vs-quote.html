Invoice vs. Receipt vs. Quote: What's the Difference?

Invoices, receipts, and quotes are easy to mix up because they all list goods or services and a price. But each one does a different job, and sending the wrong one at the wrong time confuses clients and slows down payment. Here's how to tell them apart.

The short version

The simplest way to remember the difference is to think about timing in the sales cycle:

  • A quote comes before the work — it proposes a price.
  • An invoice comes after the work — it requests payment.
  • A receipt comes after payment — it confirms money was received.

What a quote is for

A quote (sometimes called an estimate) is a proposal. You send it when a potential client asks "how much will this cost?" It lists the work you expect to do and your price, often with an expiry date so the price isn't open forever. A quote is not a request for payment — nothing is owed until the client accepts it and the work is done. Quotes set expectations and help you win the job. Create a quote or estimate here →

What an invoice is for

An invoice is a formal request for payment, sent after you've delivered the goods or completed the service. It restates what was done, shows the total due, and gives a due date and payment instructions. The invoice is what enters the client's accounts-payable process, so it's the document that actually gets you paid. If you quoted the job earlier, the invoice should match what was agreed.

What a receipt is for

A receipt is confirmation that payment has been received. You issue it after the client pays the invoice. It's proof for both sides: the client uses it for their expense records and tax deductions, and you use it to close out the transaction. A receipt typically shows the amount paid, the date of payment, and often a "Paid" marker. Create a payment receipt here →

A typical sequence

  1. Client asks for pricing → you send a quote.
  2. Client approves → you do the work.
  3. Work is done → you send an invoice.
  4. Client pays → you send a receipt.

Not every job uses all three. A quick freelance gig might skip the quote, and small cash sales might only need a receipt. But knowing the role of each document means you'll always send the right one.
