Net 30 Payment Terms Explained (Net 15, Net 60 & Due on Receipt)

"Net 30" is one of the most common phrases on an invoice — and one of the most misunderstood. Payment terms simply tell your client how long they have to pay. Choosing the right ones has a direct effect on your cash flow. Here's what each term means, the trade-offs, and how to state them so there's zero ambiguity.

What does "Net 30" mean?

Net 30 means the full ("net") amount of the invoice is due within 30 calendar days. The "net" signals that the total is payable without deductions, and the number is the count of days the client has to pay. So an invoice issued on June 1 with Net 30 terms is generally due by July 1.

One subtlety trips people up: the 30 days usually count from the invoice date, but some companies count from the delivery date or from the end of the month in which they received the invoice. Because that starting point isn't universal, the safest practice is to print the actual due date on the invoice alongside the term.

The common terms at a glance

TermWhat it meansBest for
Due on ReceiptPayment is expected immediately, as soon as the invoice arrives.Small jobs, new clients, one-off work, tight cash flow.
Net 15Full amount due within 15 days of the invoice date.Freelancers and small businesses wanting faster payment.
Net 30Full amount due within 30 days of the invoice date.Standard business-to-business work; larger clients.
Net 60Full amount due within 60 days of the invoice date.Large corporate or government clients that require it.

How payment terms affect your cash flow

Every day between finishing work and getting paid is a day you've effectively financed your client's purchase. Longer terms mean you're floating that cost for longer. If you deliver a $5,000 project on Net 60, you may not see that money for two full months — during which you still pay your own rent, software, and subcontractors.

For a small business or freelancer, this gap can be the difference between a comfortable month and a stressful one. Shorter terms pull cash in faster and reduce the risk of a client's slow accounting cycle becoming your problem. The trade-off: larger clients often have fixed payment processes, and insisting on Due on Receipt may not be realistic with them.

Pros and cons of longer vs. shorter terms

Shorter terms (Due on Receipt, Net 15)

  • Pro: Cash arrives faster and predictably.
  • Pro: Sets a prompt-payment expectation from day one.
  • Con: Can feel aggressive to large clients with monthly payment runs.

Longer terms (Net 30, Net 60)

  • Pro: Matches how big companies prefer to pay, which can win you the work.
  • Pro: Looks standard and professional in B2B contexts.
  • Con: You wait longer for money and carry more cash-flow risk.

When to use each term

There's no single right answer — match the term to the situation:

  • New or unproven clients: lean toward Due on Receipt or Net 15, or ask for a deposit up front.
  • Small or fixed-price jobs: Due on Receipt keeps things simple.
  • Established B2B relationships: Net 30 is the familiar default.
  • Large corporations or government: Net 60 may be required by their procurement rules — build the wait into your pricing.

Early-payment discounts (like 2/10 Net 30)

If you want the security of longer terms but the cash of shorter ones, offer an early-payment discount. The classic notation is 2/10 Net 30, which reads as: "take 2% off if you pay within 10 days; otherwise the full amount is due in 30 days." On a $2,000 invoice, that's a $40 discount in exchange for getting paid three weeks sooner.

Many clients with healthy cash reserves will happily take the discount, which speeds up your incoming cash without you having to chase anyone. Just make sure the discount is small enough that it's worth it to you — and state both the percentage and the deadline plainly.

How to state your terms clearly on an invoice

Vague terms invite slow payment. To remove every excuse, your invoice should:

  • Name the term (e.g., "Payment terms: Net 30").
  • Show the exact due date ("Due: July 1, 2026") — this beats "Net 30" because it removes any debate about when the clock started.
  • Spell out any early-payment discount and its deadline.
  • State any late fee (for example, "1.5% per month on overdue balances").
  • Include how to pay right on the invoice.

Whenever you can, agree the terms before the work starts — in a proposal or contract — so the invoice merely confirms what both sides already expect.

A quick worked example

Imagine you're a freelancer who finishes a $4,000 branding project on June 1. Compare how the same job plays out under three different terms:

  • Due on Receipt: you send the invoice June 1 and, with a prompt client, see the $4,000 within a few days — ideal when you have bills due that month.
  • Net 30: the invoice is due July 1. The client treats that as the deadline, so the money typically lands at the end of the window. You've financed a month of waiting.
  • 2/10 Net 30: you offer a 2% discount for paying by June 11. The client takes it, you receive $3,920 in ten days, and you trade $80 for three weeks of earlier cash.

Notice that the work, the client, and the price are identical — only the terms changed, yet the impact on your bank balance is dramatically different. That's why terms deserve a deliberate choice rather than a copy-paste habit.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Writing only "Net 30" with no date. Always pair the term with the calendar due date so there's nothing to interpret.
  • Defaulting to Net 30 for everyone. A brand-new client and a long-standing corporate account don't need the same terms.
  • Surprising the client at invoice time. Terms should be agreed up front, not revealed after the work is done.
  • Offering a discount that's too generous. Keep early-payment discounts small (1-2%) so speeding up cash doesn't quietly erode your margin.
