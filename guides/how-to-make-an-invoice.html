How to Make an Invoice: A Simple Step-by-Step Guide

An invoice is just a clear request for payment. If you can write down who you are, what you did, and how much it costs, you can make a professional invoice in a few minutes. Here is the whole process, step by step.

1. Start with a header that identifies the document

At the very top, write the word "Invoice" so there is no confusion about what the document is. This matters more than it sounds: a client's accounts team sorts incoming mail by document type, and a clearly labeled invoice gets routed for payment faster than a vague "statement" or "bill."

Next to or below the title, add a unique invoice number (for example, INV-0001). Numbering every invoice in sequence keeps your records clean, makes it easy to reference a specific job later, and is expected by almost every accounting system.

2. Add your details and your client's details

Under a "From" heading, list your business or personal name, address, email, and phone number. Under a "Bill to" heading, do the same for your client. If you are invoicing a company, address it to the right department or contact person — invoices sent to a generic inbox are the ones that get lost.

3. Set the dates and payment terms

Include two dates: the issue date (when you created the invoice) and the due date (when payment is expected). Spelling out a due date is far more effective than writing "Net 30," because it removes any ambiguity about when the clock started. If you offer a discount for early payment or charge a fee for late payment, state it here.

4. List what you are billing for

This is the heart of the invoice. Add one line for each product or service, and for each line include:

  • A short, specific description ("Logo design — 3 concepts + revisions" beats "Design work").
  • The quantity — hours, units, or a flat "1" for a fixed-price item.
  • The unit price.
  • The line total (quantity × unit price).

Specific descriptions reduce back-and-forth questions and make the invoice easier to approve.

5. Calculate the totals

Add up all the line totals to get the subtotal. If you charge sales tax or VAT, apply the correct rate and show the tax as its own line. Then show the total amount due in bold — this is the single number your client cares about most, so make it easy to find.

6. Add payment instructions and notes

Tell the client exactly how to pay you: bank transfer details, a payment link, or accepted methods. A short, friendly note ("Thank you for your business!") and a clear late-payment policy round out a professional invoice.

7. Review, save as PDF, and send

Double-check the numbers and the spelling of names, then save the invoice as a PDF. PDF is the right format to send because it looks identical on every device and can't be accidentally edited. Email it to your client, or attach it to your project-management tool, and keep a copy for your own records.
