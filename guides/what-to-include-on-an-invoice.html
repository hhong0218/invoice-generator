What to Include on an Invoice: A Required-Fields Checklist

A good invoice answers every question a client might have before they pay — without them needing to email you back. Here is exactly what belongs on an invoice, why each field matters, and the small details that separate a professional invoice from a sloppy one.

The essential fields

Almost every invoice, in any country and any industry, includes these core elements:

  • The word "Invoice." A clear label so the document is unmistakable.
  • A unique invoice number. Used to track and reference the invoice.
  • Your details. Business or personal name, address, email, and phone.
  • The client's details. Their name, company, and billing address.
  • Issue date and due date. When you sent it and when payment is expected.
  • Itemized line items. Description, quantity, and unit price for each.
  • Subtotal, tax, and total due. The math, laid out clearly.
  • Payment terms and instructions. How and when to pay.

Invoice numbering done right

Your invoice number is a small field that prevents big headaches. Use a consistent format and never reuse a number. Common schemes include:

  • Sequential: INV-0001, INV-0002, INV-0003.
  • Date-based: 2024-001, 2024-002 (resetting each year).
  • Client-based: ACME-001, ACME-002 for one client.

Pick one scheme and stick with it. Sequential numbering also makes it obvious if an invoice ever goes missing, which is useful at tax time.

Payment terms that actually get you paid

"Payment terms" simply means when and how you expect to be paid. Be explicit:

  • State a real due date ("Due September 30, 2024") rather than only "Net 30."
  • List accepted payment methods and include the details — bank account, payment link, or platform.
  • Spell out late fees or early-payment discounts if you use them, so there are no surprises.

Tax fields

If you are required to charge sales tax or VAT, show the tax rate and the tax amount as separate lines, and include your tax registration number if your jurisdiction requires it. Even when no tax applies, it's good practice to make that explicit so the client knows the total is final.

Optional fields that add polish

None of these are required, but they make your invoice look more professional and reduce friction:

  • Your logo, for instant brand recognition.
  • A purchase order (PO) number, if the client provided one — many companies won't pay without it.
  • A short thank-you note or project reference.
