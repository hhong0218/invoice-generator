Cleaning Service Invoice Template

Cleaning businesses bill in lots of ways — per visit, per hour, per room, or on a recurring schedule — and often add supplies or one-off deep cleans. This guide shows how to put together a clear cleaning invoice, and you can create one free in your browser.

Ways to price a cleaning invoice

Most cleaning invoices use one of these structures, and the right one depends on the job:

  • Flat per-visit rate. A set price for a standard clean of a known space — the simplest option for regular residential clients.
  • Hourly. Best for variable jobs or first cleans. Put your hourly rate in the unit price and hours in the quantity column, and note the team size if you charge per cleaner.
  • Per room or per square foot. Common in commercial cleaning, where pricing scales with the area covered.
  • Add-ons. Deep cleans, oven or fridge interiors, window washing, or move-out cleans listed as separate lines so the base rate stays clear.

Recurring and contract cleaning

Recurring clients — weekly homes or daily office contracts — are the backbone of a cleaning business. For these, issue an invoice on a consistent cycle (weekly, biweekly, or monthly) with the same line description, so the client always recognizes the charge. For commercial contracts, reference the contract or service agreement number on each invoice. Many cleaners offer a small discount for recurring service versus one-off bookings; show that as a discount line so the client sees the value of staying on schedule.

Supplies, equipment, and expenses

If you supply your own cleaning products and equipment, that’s usually built into your rate. But specialty supplies for a particular job — a specific floor finish, carpet shampoo, or extra consumables for a large site — can be itemized separately. Be clear and consistent: clients dislike surprise supply charges, so if you bill them, mention it when quoting and list them plainly on the invoice.

Payment terms for cleaning services

Residential cleaners are often paid on the day of service or within a few days, so a short window like net–7 or “due on receipt” is normal. Commercial contracts more often run net–15 or net–30. Offer convenient payment methods — card, bank transfer, or a payment link — because the easier it is to pay, the faster you’re paid. Note your accepted methods and any late fee in the invoice.

Look professional, get paid on time

A tidy, branded invoice reassures clients that you run a real business — which matters when you’re entrusted with keys and access. Make sure each invoice has a unique number, the service date, your business and the client’s details, itemized lines, subtotal, tax if you charge it, and the total due. Frequently asked questions

Should I charge per hour or a flat rate?

Flat per-visit rates work well for regular clients with a known space, while hourly is better for first cleans and variable jobs. Many cleaners quote flat rates for recurring residential work and bill hourly for one-off deep cleans.

How do I invoice recurring cleaning clients?

Issue an invoice on a consistent cycle — weekly, biweekly, or monthly — with the same line description each time so the charge is instantly recognizable. For commercial accounts, reference the service contract number on every invoice.

Should cleaning supplies be a separate line?

Routine products are usually built into your rate. Specialty supplies for a specific job can be itemized, but mention any supply charges when you quote so they’re never a surprise on the invoice.

What payment terms are normal for cleaners?

Residential work is often due on receipt or within about seven days, while commercial contracts commonly run net–15 or net–30. Offering card or payment-link options helps you get paid faster.
