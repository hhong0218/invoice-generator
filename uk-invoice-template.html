UK Invoice Template

UK invoices follow clearer rules than many countries — especially once you’re VAT-registered, when specific fields become mandatory. This guide explains what every UK invoice needs and lets you create a compliant one free in your browser.

What a UK invoice must include

For a basic (non-VAT) invoice, HMRC expects a clear, professional document. Once you are VAT-registered, a full VAT invoice has specific legal requirements:

  • A unique, sequential invoice number (no gaps).
  • Your business name and address, and your VAT registration number.
  • The invoice date and the time of supply (tax point) if different.
  • The customer’s name and address.
  • A description of the goods or services supplied.
  • The rate of VAT and the amount charged per line, plus the total excluding VAT, the VAT amount, and the total including VAT — in pounds sterling.

If you are not VAT-registered, you must not charge VAT or show a VAT number, but you should still issue a clear numbered invoice.

UK VAT in brief

The standard UK VAT rate is currently 20%, with a reduced 5% rate and a 0% (zero) rate for certain goods and services. You must register for VAT once your taxable turnover exceeds the HMRC threshold (and you can register voluntarily below it). Registered businesses add VAT to their invoices, reclaim VAT on eligible purchases, and file VAT returns — usually quarterly — under Making Tax Digital. Show the VAT rate and amount as a separate line so the customer can reclaim their input VAT.

Sole traders and limited companies

A sole trader invoices under their own or trading name and includes their address; there’s no requirement to show a company number. A limited company should include its registered company name and number, and if VAT-registered, its VAT number too. Both follow the same numbering and VAT rules. Many small UK freelancers operate below the VAT threshold and simply issue numbered invoices without VAT.

Payment terms in the UK

Common UK terms are 14 or 30 days from the invoice date. Under the Late Payment of Commercial Debts legislation, businesses are entitled to charge statutory interest and a fixed recovery fee on overdue B2B invoices — a useful backstop even if you rarely invoke it. State your terms and accepted payment methods clearly. Bank transfer (Faster Payments) is the norm; include your sort code and account number, or a payment link, to make settlement easy.

Frequently asked questions

What must a UK VAT invoice include?

A VAT-registered business must show a unique sequential number, its name, address and VAT number, the invoice date and tax point, the customer’s details, a description of the supply, the VAT rate and amount per line, and the totals excluding VAT, the VAT amount, and the total including VAT.

Do I charge VAT if I’m not registered?

No. If you are not VAT-registered you must not charge VAT or display a VAT number. You should still issue a clear, sequentially numbered invoice. You only register once your taxable turnover crosses the HMRC threshold, or voluntarily before that.

What is the standard UK VAT rate?

The standard rate is currently 20%. There is also a reduced rate of 5% and a zero rate for certain goods and services. Show the rate and amount as a separate VAT line on the invoice.

Can I charge interest on late UK invoices?

Yes. Under the Late Payment of Commercial Debts rules, businesses can charge statutory interest and a fixed recovery fee on overdue B2B invoices. State your payment terms on the invoice so any late charge is backed up.
