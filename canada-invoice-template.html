Canada Invoice Template

Canadian invoicing centres on getting sales tax right — GST, HST, or GST plus PST depending on the province — and on the details the CRA expects so your clients can claim input tax credits. This guide explains it and lets you build a compliant Canadian invoice free in your browser.

What a Canadian invoice should include

For your client to claim input tax credits, the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) expects a sales-tax invoice to show:

  • Your business name and a unique invoice number.
  • The invoice date.
  • Your GST/HST registration number (your Business Number) if you’re registered — this is required for clients to recover the tax.
  • The client’s name (for larger invoices).
  • A description of the goods or services.
  • The amount before tax, the GST/HST (and PST where applicable) shown separately, and the total — in Canadian dollars.

GST, HST, and PST by province

Canada layers sales tax differently across the country, which is the part most people get wrong:

  • GST (5%) applies federally. In provinces that haven’t harmonized, you charge GST and the province may add its own PST (or QST in Quebec) as a separate line.
  • HST combines federal and provincial tax into one rate in harmonized provinces (for example Ontario and the Atlantic provinces), so you charge a single HST line.
  • The rate you charge generally depends on the province where the supply is made or the customer is located — the “place of supply” rules.

Show each applicable tax on its own line so the client can identify exactly what they paid.

Registering for GST/HST

You must register for GST/HST once your revenue exceeds the CRA small-supplier threshold over four consecutive quarters (and you can register voluntarily below it). Until you’re registered, you don’t charge GST/HST and shouldn’t show a registration number. Once registered, you collect the tax, claim input tax credits on business purchases, and remit the difference to the CRA on your filing schedule.

Payment terms and getting paid

Net–15 and net–30 are standard in Canada. Interac e-Transfer is extremely common for smaller invoices and is fast and low-cost; bank transfer (EFT) suits larger B2B payments, and cards or payment links work for quick turnarounds. State a clear due date, your accepted methods, and any late-payment terms on the invoice.

Frequently asked questions

Do I show GST/HST and PST separately?

Yes. List each applicable tax on its own line — for example GST plus PST in a non-harmonized province, or a single HST line in a harmonized one — so your client can see exactly what tax they paid and claim the correct input tax credits.

When do I need a GST/HST number?

Once your revenue crosses the CRA small-supplier threshold over four consecutive quarters you must register; you can also register voluntarily earlier. Until registered, you don’t charge GST/HST or show a number. After registering, include the number so clients can recover the tax.

What’s the difference between GST and HST?

GST is the 5% federal tax charged in provinces that haven’t harmonized, often alongside a separate provincial PST. HST is a single combined federal-plus-provincial rate used in harmonized provinces like Ontario. Which you charge depends on the place-of-supply rules.

What currency and terms are standard in Canada?

Invoices are in Canadian dollars, with net–15 or net–30 terms being typical. Interac e-Transfer is popular for smaller amounts and EFT for larger B2B payments. State a clear due date and accepted methods on the invoice.
