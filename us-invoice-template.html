US Invoice Template

Invoicing in the United States is flexible — there’s no mandated government format for most businesses — but there are clear conventions around sales tax, payment terms, and the fields clients expect. This guide explains them and lets you build a compliant US invoice free in your browser.

What a US invoice should include

There is no federally required invoice format for ordinary business-to-business or business-to-consumer sales in the US, but a professional invoice that gets paid promptly includes:

  • The word “Invoice” and a unique invoice number.
  • Your business name, address, and contact details — and, for B2B, often your EIN if the client requests it for their records.
  • The client’s name and billing address, plus any purchase order (PO) number they provided — many US companies won’t pay without a matching PO.
  • Issue date and a clear due date.
  • Itemized line items with quantity and unit price.
  • Subtotal, sales tax, and total due in US dollars.

US sales tax basics

The US has no national VAT. Instead, sales tax is set at the state and often local level, so the rate — and whether a sale is taxable at all — depends on where the customer is and what you sell. Many services are exempt while physical goods are taxable, but this varies widely by state. If you have nexus (a tax obligation) in the customer’s state, you generally charge that state’s combined rate and remit it. When tax applies, show the rate and amount as a separate line. When it doesn’t, it’s fine to leave tax at zero — just make the total clearly final.

Payment terms and conventions

US invoices commonly use “net” terms — net–15, net–30, or net–60 — meaning payment is due that many days after the invoice date. Net–30 is a frequent default for B2B. Smaller vendors and freelancers often use net–15 or “due on receipt.” Stating a concrete calendar due date alongside the net term reduces confusion. Late fees (for example 1.5% per month) are common but should be agreed in advance and noted on the invoice.

Getting paid in the US

ACH bank transfer is the workhorse of US B2B payments — low cost and reliable. Checks still appear, especially with larger or older companies. Cards and payment links (Stripe, PayPal, and similar) are popular for smaller invoices and faster turnaround, though they carry processing fees. List your accepted methods and the details clearly so the client can pay without emailing you back.

Frequently asked questions

Is there a required invoice format in the US?

No. For most US businesses there’s no government-mandated invoice template. You simply need a clear, professional document with a number, your and the client’s details, itemized lines, dates, and the total. Following common conventions helps you get paid faster.

Do I have to charge sales tax on a US invoice?

Only if you have a tax obligation (nexus) in the customer’s state and the sale is taxable there. Rates are set by state and locality, and many services are exempt. When tax applies, show it as a separate line at the correct combined rate.

What do net–15 and net–30 mean?

They specify when payment is due — 15 or 30 days after the invoice date. Net–30 is a common B2B default; freelancers often use net–15 or “due on receipt.” Adding the actual due date avoids ambiguity.

Do I need to put my EIN on the invoice?

It’s not required for most sales, but B2B clients may request it for their records or 1099 reporting. You can include it if asked. Sole proprietors without an EIN can use their other identifying details.
