Photographer Invoice Template

Photography invoices need to cover more than time behind the camera — there’s pre-production, editing, deliverables, travel, and image licensing. This guide explains how to itemize a shoot so clients understand exactly what they’re paying for, and lets you build the invoice free in your browser.

Itemizing a photography invoice

Clients see a photographer’s day rate and often assume it covers everything. It usually doesn’t. Editing alone can take longer than the shoot, and licensing is a genuine part of the value you deliver. Spelling these out as separate lines isn’t nickel-and-diming — it educates the client and justifies your price.

A thorough photographer invoice typically lists:

  • Session or coverage fee — a half-day, full-day, or hourly rate for the shoot itself.
  • Pre-production — location scouting, planning calls, or styling, if billed separately.
  • Post-production / editing — culling and retouching, often priced per image or per hour.
  • Deliverables — the number of edited high-resolution images, a gallery, prints, or an album.
  • Licensing or usage rights — especially for commercial work, where the fee scales with how and where the images will be used.
  • Travel and expenses — mileage, equipment rental, or second-shooter fees.

Day rates vs. licensing

Two pricing models dominate photography. A day-rate (or session) model charges for the photographer’s time and a set deliverable — common for weddings, portraits, and events. A licensing model, standard in commercial and editorial work, charges a creative fee plus a usage license that depends on the medium, territory, and duration. For commercial clients, make the license explicit on the invoice: what the images may be used for, where, and for how long. Re-licensing for new uses is then a clean follow-up invoice.

Deposits, retainers, and cancellations

For booked dates — weddings especially — a non-refundable retainer secures the date and is standard. Bill it as its own invoice when the client books, then show it as a paid deposit line on the final invoice so the balance is obvious. Note your cancellation and rescheduling policy in the invoice or contract so there’s no dispute if plans change.

Print and product sales

If you sell prints, albums, or wall art, these are physical products and may be taxable even where your service isn’t. List each product with quantity and unit price, and apply the correct sales-tax rate. Keeping products on separate lines from your service fee makes your bookkeeping and tax filing much simpler at year end.

Frequently asked questions

Should licensing be a separate line on my invoice?

For commercial and editorial work, yes. Separating the creative fee from the usage license makes the value clear and lets you re-license for new uses later with a simple follow-up invoice. For weddings and portraits, a single coverage fee with a personal-use license is usually fine.

How much deposit should a photographer charge?

A retainer of 25–50% of the total is common to secure a date, and it’s often non-refundable. Invoice it when the client books and show it as a paid deposit on the final invoice so the remaining balance is clear.

Do I charge sales tax on photography?

It varies by jurisdiction. Physical products like prints and albums are frequently taxable even where the service is not, and many places tax the whole package if a tangible item is delivered. Check your local rules and apply the correct rate on the invoice.

When should I send the final invoice?

Send it as galleries or albums are ready to deliver — clients are most motivated to pay then. Prompt invoicing with a clear due date is one of the simplest ways to get paid faster.
