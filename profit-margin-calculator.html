What is a profit margin calculator?

This calculator works out how much of your selling price is actual profit, using your cost and price per unit. Toggle between two modes: "I know my price" calculates the margin and markup you're already getting, while "I know my target margin" works backward to tell you the exact price you need to charge to hit a specific margin goal.

How it's calculated

The two formulas below use the standard, universally accepted definitions of margin and markup:

  • Profit margin (%) = (Price − Cost) ÷ Price × 100 — profit as a percentage of the selling price.
  • Markup (%) = (Price − Cost) ÷ Cost × 100 — profit as a percentage of the cost.
  • Required price (from cost + target margin) = Cost ÷ (1 − Target margin ÷ 100).

Worked example: a product costs $60 and sells for $100. Profit is $40. Margin = $40 ÷ $100 = 40%. Markup = $40 ÷ $60 = 66.7%. Same $40 profit, two different percentages — because they're measured against two different bases.

Profit margin vs. markup: what's the difference?

Margin and markup are the single most confused pair of numbers in small-business pricing, because they use the same profit dollar amount but divide it by different things:

The mistake people make most often: assuming a "50% markup" and a "50% margin" produce the same price. They don't. A 50% markup on a $60 cost gives a $90 price (margin ≈ 33%). A 50% margin on a $60 cost requires a $120 price (markup = 100%). Always double-check which one you — or a client, spreadsheet, or supplier — actually mean.

Why this matters for pricing

If you set prices using markup but report profitability using margin (or vice versa), your numbers can look far healthier or worse than reality. A 100% markup sounds impressive, but it's "only" a 50% margin — still good, but the two labels can create very different expectations in a conversation with a partner, investor, or accountant. Knowing both, and which one a given context refers to, avoids costly misunderstandings.

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between margin and markup?

Margin divides profit by the selling price; markup divides profit by the cost. For the same $40 profit on a $60 cost / $100 price item, margin is 40% and markup is 66.7%. Margin is always the smaller number of the two (for a profitable price).

How do I calculate profit margin from cost and price?

Subtract cost from price to get profit, then divide profit by price and multiply by 100: Margin % = (Price − Cost) ÷ Price × 100.

What price should I charge for a target margin?

Use Price = Cost ÷ (1 − Target margin ÷ 100). For example, to hit a 25% margin on a $60 cost: 60 ÷ (1 − 0.25) = 60 ÷ 0.75 = $80.

Is a higher margin always better?

Higher margin generally means more profit per sale, but the right margin depends on your industry, competition, and volume. Retail and manufacturing often run thinner margins with high volume; services and software often support much higher margins. Compare against typical figures for your specific industry rather than a single universal target.
